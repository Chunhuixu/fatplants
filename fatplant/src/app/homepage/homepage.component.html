Increasing seed oil content by plant breeding has resulted in trade-offs or penalties with respect to protein content, seed size, and seed set.

The molecular basis for this impasse is mostly speculative. Use of current global profiling approaches to better understand both the metabolic consequences of higher oil and the basis for reduced yield must also deal with off-target genetic mutations (even in near-isogenic lines), ultimately confounding cause-effect interpretations. We propose a diverse, integrated strategy to study the consequences of higher lipid production by studying transgenic plants specifically engineered to produce higher seed oil.

Breakthroughs in the regulations of the committed step of de novo fatty acid synthesis (FAS) have been leveraged to produce oilseeds with substantially higher oil content. Prior to this time, changes in oil content have been minor, which has made the identification of small differences in metabolism difficult to study. We are now in a position to address recalcitrant questions about the metabolic consequences for enhancing seed oil.

Project Aims

Establish regulatory features in oil-for-protein and oil-for-seed yield trade-offs by measuring: transcripts, polysomal loading (i.e. dynamically translated transcript levels), proteins, and metabolites during the three stages of seed development in engineered high-oil lines as compared to wild type(WT).

Measure the oil to protein trade-off through analysis of carbon flux into central and fatty acid metabolism in high-oil as compared to WT germplasm.

Assess acyl-lipid biosynthetic turnover rates as well as bottlenecks within the lipid metabolic network through targeted flux analysis.

Develop a “one-stop-shop” community web resource for all data pertaining to enhancing oil content in plants and leverage data generated from this project with curated forms of public data such as other funded web sites and literature.

Improve STEM education by providing multidisciplinary training to college students, graduates, postgraduates, and high school teachers.
